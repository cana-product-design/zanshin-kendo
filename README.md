# Zanshin

## Why Zanshin exists

Zanshin began as a personal question: what does it mean to keep practising Kendo when your body no longer lets you train as it once did?

After years of practice, I found myself navigating the reality that many Kenshi eventually meet: ageing, recurring injuries, changing energy, and the need to adapt rather than simply push harder. For some of us, those limits are temporary. For others, they are permanent. Neither should mean that Kendo has to end.

This project is an exploration of **Kendo for life**. It is not built around chasing competitive performance or recreating the intensity of youth. Its purpose is to support a more sustainable relationship with practice: one that respects the body you have today, makes room for recovery and mental wellbeing, and helps you keep a connection to the path over decades.

The first version grew from a desire to create training guidance that responds to a person's own context—their goals, environment, experience, and limitations—instead of treating every practitioner as if they had the same capacity. It is also a small expression of gratitude to the sensei and dojo community who have made Kendo feel larger than training alone.

There is a Zen thread running through that intention. Zanshin is not only about completing a session or earning progress; it is about attention, acceptance, and continuing with care. The long-term hope is to make space for guidance from sensei, for the details of practice such as *seme*, *ashi-sabaki*, and *kirikaeshi*, and for quieter practices that help Kenshi meet themselves where they are.

Zanshin is still a work in progress, but its direction is clear: help more people remain in Kendo—not by ignoring limitation, but by making adaptation part of the practice.

> Zanshin is a training companion, not medical advice. Any training plan should be adapted with qualified medical and coaching guidance when injury or health concerns are involved.
